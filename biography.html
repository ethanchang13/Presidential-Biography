The Life of JFK

From Massachusetts to the White House

Early Life

John Fitzgerald Kennedy was born on May 29, 1917, in Brookline, Massachusetts. He was the second of nine children born to Joseph P. Kennedy Sr. and Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy. His father was a successful businessman and later served as the U.S. Ambassador to the United Kingdom, while his mother was the daughter of Boston's former mayor.

Kennedy grew up in a highly competitive environment, with high expectations set by his parents. The Kennedy family was affluent and influential, providing John with many advantages in life while also instilling in him a strong sense of public service.

Education

Kennedy attended several prestigious schools during his early education, including the Dexter School in Brookline. He later attended the Choate School and Princeton University briefly before transferring to Harvard University, where he graduated in 1940 with a Bachelor of Arts in government and international relations.

While at Harvard, Kennedy wrote his senior thesis on "Appeasement in Munich," which was later published as the book "Why England Slept." This work demonstrated his early interest in international affairs and leadership during crisis.

Military Service

Following the U.S. entry into World War II, Kennedy joined the United States Naval Reserve in 1941. Despite medical issues that could have exempted him from service, Kennedy was determined to serve his country.

Kennedy was assigned to command a patrol torpedo boat, PT-109, in the South Pacific. On August 2, 1943, his boat was rammed by a Japanese destroyer and split in half. Two of his crew were killed, but Kennedy led the survivors, swimming for hours to reach a small island. He even towed one injured crew member by clinching the strap of the man's life jacket between his teeth.

For his leadership and courage, Kennedy received the Navy and Marine Corps Medal, the highest non-combat decoration awarded for heroism, as well as the Purple Heart for injuries sustained in the incident. This experience profoundly influenced his views on courage, leadership, and public service.

Political Career

After the war, Kennedy entered politics, winning election to the U.S. House of Representatives in 1946, representing a working-class district in Boston. He served three terms in the House before successfully running for the U.S. Senate in 1952.

As a senator, Kennedy was known for his independent thinking and his work on labor issues and foreign policy. He gained national attention with the publication of his book "Profiles in Courage," which won the Pulitzer Prize for Biography in 1957. The book profiled eight U.S. senators who had taken courageous and principled stands despite tremendous political opposition.

In 1960, Kennedy launched his campaign for the presidency against Republican Vice President Richard Nixon. The election was one of the closest in U.S. history, with Kennedy winning the popular vote by less than 0.2%. A key moment in the campaign was the first-ever televised presidential debate, where Kennedy's youthful appearance and confidence contrasted with Nixon's tired and uncomfortable demeanor.

Personal Life

In 1953, Kennedy married Jacqueline Bouvier, a writer and photographer who would become one of the most iconic First Ladies in American history. The couple had four children, though only two—Caroline and John Jr.—survived infancy.

Throughout his life, Kennedy struggled with various health problems, including chronic back pain and Addison's disease, though these issues were largely hidden from the public during his political career.

Despite his health challenges, Kennedy cultivated an image of youth, vigor, and athleticism, which contributed to his appeal as a political figure and helped define a new era in American culture and politics.
